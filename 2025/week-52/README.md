# Week 52, 2025 — Dec 22–31

> **Key Takeaway:** 2025 closes with three moves that summarise the year's dominant forces. Nvidia acquired Groq ($20B) to lock down the inference chip market before LPUs could erode its GPU moat. Meta acquired Manus AI ($2B+) to own the autonomous agent layer heading into 2026. And OpenAI's $1.5M average employee compensation — 34x the pre-IPO benchmark — shows how extreme the talent war has become. The golden handcuffs are real, and so is the underlying fragility they reflect.

---

## 📰 News

- **Nvidia acquires Groq for $20B** — Groq's LPU (Language Processing Unit) technology was emerging as a credible threat to GPU dominance in high-speed inference. Nvidia bought the company's assets (not the full company, likely to sidestep regulatory scrutiny) on Christmas Eve. Defensive consolidation: acquire the competition before it erodes your moat. *(Dec 2025)*
- **Meta acquires Manus AI for $2B+** — Singapore-based startup known for the first "general-purpose" AI agent capable of autonomous task completion. Zuckerberg's bet that autonomous agents are the 2026 battleground. *(Dec 2025)*
- **OpenAI average employee compensation: $1.5M in stock** — WSJ report. 34x higher than the average pre-IPO tech company over the last 25 years. Reflects extreme talent competition and the need to retain people who could walk out and raise $300M the next day (see LeCun, W46; Periodic Labs, W40). *(Dec 2025)*
- **John Carreyrou et al. sue six major AI companies over pirated training data** — The journalist who took down Theranos, joined by other authors, filed against six major AI companies alleging training on pirated book repositories (Z-Library). Extends the copyright lawsuit wave from W36 (Anthropic settlement), W46 (German court ruling), and W48 (California suicide case) into a broader, multi-defendant front. *(Dec 2025)*

## 💬 Opinion & Analysis

- Nvidia buying Groq's assets rather than the company is the 2025 M&A move that deserves the most regulatory scrutiny. LPUs were one of the few credible hardware alternatives to GPU-based inference — buying the technology while structuring the deal to avoid merger review removes a competitive option from the market at exactly the moment when inference efficiency matters most.
- Meta's Manus acquisition closes the loop on a pattern running all year: every major AI lab is racing to own the autonomous agent layer. OpenAI has AgentKit (W41) and Sky (W43); Anthropic has Claude Code (W48) and Claude Opus 4.5 tool-use (W48); Meta now has Manus. The agent wars are 2026's version of the model wars.
- OpenAI's $1.5M average stock compensation is simultaneously a sign of strength (we can afford this) and fragility (we need this to retain people). At 34x the pre-IPO benchmark, it implies OpenAI believes it will be worth multiples of any comparable company at IPO — or that the talent risk of not paying this is existential. Probably both.
- The Carreyrou lawsuit adds a high-credibility journalist with a track record of sustained, successful investigative campaigns against a tech company. The Theranos comparison isn't perfect, but the pattern — charismatic founder, extraordinary valuations, legal and ethical corner-cutting under competitive pressure — is one that observers will draw regardless.
- Closing the year: 2025 started with DeepSeek R1 (W05) shaking the assumption that frontier AI required closed, hyperscale compute. It ends with Nvidia spending $20B to eliminate an inference chip threat, OpenAI paying $1.5M per employee to stay competitive, and Meta buying a general-purpose agent startup. The year's arc is: open disruption → massive capital response → consolidation. The question for 2026 is whether consolidation holds or whether the next DeepSeek-level disruption is already being built in a garage somewhere.
