# Week 33, 2025 — Aug 18–23

> **Key Takeaway:** Two stories define this week's tension: DeepSeek V3.1 dropped open-source with 658B parameters and state-of-the-art coding benchmarks — while OpenAI seeks a $500B valuation amid complaints about GPT-5's quality. The gap between open-source momentum and closed-source valuations is becoming harder to justify. Meanwhile, fake AI journalist "Margaux Blanchard" fooled Wired and Business Insider, making the "AI slop" threat concrete.

---

## 📰 News

- **OpenAI in talks for $6B share sale at $500B valuation** — SoftBank and Thrive reportedly circling. Critics note growing complaints about GPT-5's diminished quality. Raises questions about whether valuation can sustain if product reputation continues to slip. *(Aug 2025)*
- **Fake AI persona "Margaux Blanchard" dupes Wired and Business Insider** — AI-generated articles with fabricated details (fake Minecraft wedding, fictional Colorado town) published under a fake byline at two respected outlets. Both forced to delete the articles. Ivo's take: if major newsrooms can't filter AI slop, it could be the final nail for journalism's already fragile credibility. *(Aug 2025)*
- **Google DeepMind + Kaggle launch Game Arena** — AI systems compete in strategy games (chess, Go, custom environments) rather than static benchmarks. Forces models to plan, adapt, and learn under pressure. Potential new gold standard for testing general intelligence. *(Aug 2025)*
- **MIT Tech Review: Should AI flatter, fix, or inform?** — Philosophical question with direct design consequences. How AI should behave encodes values and shapes behavior — a question that will play out for decades and differs by company and market. *(Aug 2025)*

## 💬 Opinion & Analysis

- DeepSeek V3.1 at 658B parameters open-source is another data point in the clearest trend of 2025: performance that was once a proprietary moat is becoming a public good. The closed labs' pricing power depends on this trend stopping — and it shows no sign of stopping.
- The Margaux Blanchard story is the concrete version of the AI slop fear. Not a hypothetical — actual articles, actual deletion, actual credibility damage at outlets that are supposed to be guardrails of information quality.
- Google's Game Arena is the right answer to the evaluation crisis flagged in W26. Dynamic adversarial environments can't be overfitted to the way static benchmarks can. If it becomes a standard, it changes how we measure progress.
- The "flatter vs. fix vs. inform" debate is deceptively important. The design choice of an AI's default disposition shapes millions of interactions daily. It's not neutral — it's a value encoding with societal scale.
