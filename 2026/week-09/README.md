# Week 09, 2026 — Feb 23–Mar 01

> **Key Takeaway:** The defining moment of 2026 so far: Anthropic walked away from a US Department of Defense contract because the government refused to pledge not to use AI for mass surveillance or fully autonomous weaponry. Hours later, OpenAI stepped in and signed the deal — with what critics called "regressive" safety terms. The "tale of two Sams" (Altman filling the vacuum Amodei refused) crystallises the divergence in values between the two leading AI labs into a single, concrete event.

---

## 📰 News

- **Anthropic walks away from DoD contract** — Anthropic demanded specific ethical guarantees: pledge not to use AI for mass surveillance or fully autonomous weaponry. The US government refused, maintaining its right to use AI in those areas. Anthropic declined to sign. *(Mar 2026)*
- **OpenAI signs the DoD deal hours later** — Stepped into the vacuum Anthropic left. Deal features what critics describe as "regressive" safety terms. Positions OpenAI as the government's AI partner of choice and draws immediate firestorm of criticism. Follows the mission statement change (W08) and alignment team dissolution (W06). *(Mar 2026)*

## 💬 Opinion & Analysis

- The Anthropic/DoD moment is the most significant values-divergence event in the industry since OpenAI's for-profit conversion (W44 2025). Anthropic's refusal to sign without ethical guarantees, and OpenAI's immediate willingness to sign without them, is not a policy difference — it's a foundational difference in what the companies believe AI should be allowed to do on behalf of governments.
- OpenAI signing a government AI deal with regressive safety terms, days after removing "safety" from their mission statement (W08) and months after disbanding the alignment team (W06), is a coherent sequence. Each step individually had explanations; together they describe a company that has explicitly deprioritised safety as a constraint on commercial and strategic expansion.
- Anthropic walking away from a government contract is a costly signal. These deals are enormous in revenue terms. Refusing one for ethical reasons, when a competitor will sign it immediately, is the kind of decision that only makes sense if you genuinely believe the constraint matters. Whether that conviction holds as commercial pressure intensifies is the long-term test.
- The Anthropic agent autonomy framework (long-horizon planning, self-directed goal pursuit, tool use independence, oversight resistance) is a more important research output this week than it appears. As AI agents become embedded in enterprise workflows at 75% automation rates (W04 Economic Index), the vocabulary for measuring how autonomous and how resistant to oversight an agent is becomes a safety-critical tool. Publishing this framework while OpenAI reduces safety infrastructure is a deliberate positioning move.
- The "two Sams" framing captures the commercial logic clearly: when Anthropic creates a gap in the government market, Altman fills it. The moral question and the market question are separable — and this week showed exactly how they separate in practice.
